\(\ce{Na2S_{x}}\) Catastrophe

Me: \(\ce{Na2S_{x}}\) can be oxidized by \(\ce{NaClO}\) and turn into \(\ce{Na2SO4}\) in alkaline solution. Additionally, \(\ce{NaClO}\) is reduced, and turns into \(\ce{NaCl}\). If the ratio of \(\ce{Na2S_{x}}\) and \(\ce{NaClO}\) is known as \(1:10\). Is it possible to find \(x\)? If it is, what is the value of \(x\)?

GPT-4o:

Yes, it is possible to find \(x\), which represents the number of sulfur atoms in the polysulfide ion (\( \ce{S_x^{2-}} \)), using the provided information.

Given that the reaction involves the oxidation of \(\ce{Na2S_x}\) to \( \ce{Na2SO4} \), we can write the half-reaction for the oxidation of polysulfide:

\[\ce{S_x^{2-} -> SO4^{2-} + (x-1)S^{0} + 8e^-}\]
Here, \( x-1 \) sulfur atoms are released as elemental sulfur, and one sulfur atom from \( \ce{S_x^{2-}} \) is oxidized to sulfate (\( \ce{SO4^{2-}} \)).

For the reduction of hypochlorite (\( \ce{ClO^-} \)) to chloride (\( \ce{Cl^-} \)) in an alkaline solution:

\[\ce{ClO^- + 2e^- + H2O -> Cl^- + 2OH^-}\]
Now, to balance the overall redox reaction, consider the stoichiometric relationship between \( \ce{Na2S_x} \) and \( \ce{NaClO} \). You are told that the ratio of \( \ce{Na2S_x} \) to \( \ce{NaClO} \) is \(1:10\), meaning one molecule of \( \ce{Na2S_x} \) reacts with ten molecules of \( \ce{NaClO} \).

The oxidation of \( \ce{S_x^{2-}} \) involves the transfer of 8 electrons, while the reduction of one \( \ce{ClO^-} \) consumes \(2\) electrons. Therefore, the number of \( \ce{ClO^-} \) molecules required to balance the oxidation of one \( \ce{S_x^{2-}} \) ion is:

\[\frac{8}{2} = 4\]
However, the given ratio is \(1:10\), which indicates that the oxidation of one \( \ce{S_x^{2-}} \) ion requires \(10\) molecules of \( \ce{ClO^-} \). This means the number of electrons transferred in the oxidation of \( \ce{S_x^{2-}} \) is actually:

\[2 \times 10 = 20 \text{ electrons}\]

Since each \( \ce{S_x^{2-}} \) ion releases \(8\) electrons during oxidation, we can set up the equation:

\[8x = 20\]

Solving for \( x \):

\[x = \frac{20}{8} = 2.5\]

Thus, \( x = 2.5 \).

This suggests that the polysulfide ion is likely a mixture of \( \ce{S_2^{2-}} \) and \( \ce{S_3^{2-}} \) or that it has a fractional stoichiometry in this context, which can occur in complex polysulfide mixtures.
